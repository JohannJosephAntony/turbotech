Frequently Asked Questions

Turbo lag is the delay between pressing the accelerator and feeling the turbo's power boost. It occurs because the turbine needs time to spin up. Modern solutions include smaller turbos, variable geometry turbines, and electric assistance.

Safe boost levels depend on engine design and supporting modifications. Stock engines typically handle 6-12 PSI safely, while built engines can handle 15-30+ PSI with proper tuning and supporting modifications.

Regular oil changes with high-quality synthetic oil, allowing cool-down periods after hard driving, checking for boost leaks, and monitoring for unusual noises or excessive smoke are essential for turbo longevity.

While most engines can be turbocharged, factors like compression ratio, internal strength, and fuel system capacity determine success. Lower compression engines with strong internals are ideal candidates.
